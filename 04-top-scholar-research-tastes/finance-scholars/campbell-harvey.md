# Campbell Harvey

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Empirical finance needs both return-discovery tools and discipline against false discoveries,
multiple testing, and fragile significance.

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

## Skill 1: Treat multiple testing as a first-order threat

### Generalisable research taste

In fields with many tested predictors, treat false discovery as part of the research design problem.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- ... and the Cross-Section of Expected Returns
- Backtesting
- Factor zoo papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests examine whether reported factors survive higher significance thresholds, out-of-sample
tests, or economic filters.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames the problem as field-level credibility, not a minor robustness issue.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill whenever many hypotheses, signals, or models are tried.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that stricter thresholds can reject real but weak effects. Good taste balances Type
I and Type II errors.

### Practice exercise

List every specification choice in your project that could create hidden multiple testing.

## Skill 2: Use global evidence to test return claims

### Generalisable research taste

Check whether asset-pricing patterns travel across countries, markets, and regimes.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Expected Returns and Volatility in 135 Countries
- Emerging market return studies
- Cross-country asset pricing work

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests compare expected returns, volatility, and risk relations across many markets.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames global evidence as a way to test whether a pattern is structural or sample-
specific.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by asking whether your finance result should hold outside the U.S. and why.

### Critical boundary

The risk is cross-market comparability and data quality. International breadth requires measurement
caution.

### Practice exercise

Choose one anomaly and state whether it should exist globally or only in one institutional setting.

## Skill 3: Connect empirical discovery to research ethics

### Generalisable research taste

Treat fragile significance, p-hacking, and publication bias as methodological and ethical issues.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Backtesting
- Factor zoo papers
- Research credibility writing

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests audit how research practices affect published findings.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction often explains how standard incentives can produce misleading evidence.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when building new empirical workflows or replication standards.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that not every fragile result is misconduct; many are uncertainty.

### Practice exercise

Write a disclosure paragraph for your specification search.

## Skill 4: Write finance results with survival tests

### Generalisable research taste

Frame a result as credible only after it survives economic motivation, out-of-sample evidence,
robustness, and implementability.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Backtesting
- Expected returns studies
- Factor evaluation work

**What the papers tend to test**

The empirical tests move from discovery to survival: costs, turnover, subsamples, markets, and
theory.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction can promise not just a new predictor but a disciplined evaluation of whether it
deserves belief.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by making your results section a sequence of survival tests.

### Critical boundary

The risk is endless robustness without learning. Each test should map to a specific threat.

### Practice exercise

Build a survival-test table for a new empirical finding.

## What Not to Overgeneralize
