## One-Sentence Research Taste

Finance matters for real outcomes because intermediaries, governance, and credit frictions shape
investment, growth, and fragility.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Skill 1: Connect financial frictions to real outcomes

### Generalisable research taste

Study finance not as a self-contained market but as a mechanism affecting investment, growth,
innovation, and crises.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- The Great Reversals
- Financial development papers
- Has Financial Development Made the World Riskier?

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests link financial structure or development to industry growth, crises, or real economic
allocation.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames finance as a channel through which institutions and incentives affect real
activity.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when a finance variable matters because it changes real decisions.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is reverse causality: real growth also changes financial development.

### Practice exercise

Write the real decision affected by your finance mechanism.

## Skill 2: Use cross-country variation with institutional caution

### Generalisable research taste

Exploit international variation while explicitly discussing institutional comparability and omitted
political economy.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Capital structure international evidence
- The Great Reversals
- Financial development studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests compare countries but interpret results through legal, political, and institutional
differences.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing uses cross-country facts to generate puzzles, not as automatic causal proof.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by pairing cross-country regressions with institutional narratives and robustness.

### Critical boundary

The risk is broad cross-country overclaiming. Good taste narrows mechanisms and alternative
explanations.

### Practice exercise

For one cross-country fact, list three institutional confounds.

## Skill 3: Treat financial development as political economy

### Generalisable research taste

Ask who benefits from financial development or repression and how incumbents, politics, or crises
shape finance.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- The Great Reversals
- Financial development work
- Governance papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests examine historical reversals, institutions, and political incentives behind financial
systems.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction often asks why seemingly efficient financial arrangements do not persist or emerge
everywhere.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill in banking, capital markets, fintech, or development finance.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that political stories need evidence, not just plausibility.

### Practice exercise

Identify the coalition that benefits from one financial regulation.

## Skill 4: Write crisis arguments before the crisis is obvious

### Generalisable research taste

Notice fragile incentives and systemic risks before they become consensus.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Has Financial Development Made the World Riskier?
- Banking and liquidity papers
- Crisis-related writing

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests and theory examine incentives, risk-taking, and intermediation structures that can amplify
shocks.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames innovation and development as potentially stability-reducing, not
automatically good.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by asking how a beneficial financial innovation could create hidden tail risk.

### Critical boundary

The risk is alarmism. Good taste identifies the mechanism and conditions for fragility.

### Practice exercise

Take one financial innovation and write both its efficiency benefit and fragility channel.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
