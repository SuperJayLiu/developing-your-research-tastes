# Jeremy Stein

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Financial decisions are shaped by agency, mispricing, constraints, and time horizons; good theory
identifies which friction drives the observed behavior.

## Skill 1: Identify the friction behind financial behavior

### Generalisable research taste

Do not describe financing or investment patterns only as correlations; name the friction that makes
the behavior rational or predictable.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Market Timing and Capital Structure
- Inefficient Investment and Stock Market Valuation
- Behavioral asset pricing work

**What the papers tend to test**

The theory and tests distinguish market timing, agency, mispricing, constraints, and managerial
incentives.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction typically presents a puzzling financial behavior and then isolates the friction.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill in corporate finance, asset pricing, banking, and macro-finance.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that many frictions imply similar behavior. Tests must separate mechanisms.

### Practice exercise

Pick one corporate action and list three frictions that could explain it.

## Skill 2: Connect market prices to real corporate actions

### Generalisable research taste

Ask when stock market mispricing or valuation affects investment, issuance, mergers, or payout.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Inefficient Investment and Stock Market Valuation
- Market Timing and Capital Structure
- Behavioral corporate finance papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests link valuation measures or sentiment to real corporate decisions.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames prices as potentially causal signals or incentives, not just outcomes.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this when market conditions may change firm behavior.

### Critical boundary

The risk is reverse causality: prices may predict decisions because they reflect fundamentals.

### Practice exercise

Write a design that separates mispricing from fundamentals in one corporate decision.

## Skill 3: Write simple theory for empirical finance

### Generalisable research taste

Use small models to make empirical predictions sharp enough to test.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Behavioral Approach to Asset Pricing
- Corporate finance theory papers
- Financial stability work

**What the papers tend to test**

The theory produces predictions about cross-sectional or time-series behavior under specific
frictions.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction makes clear what empirical pattern the model organizes.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill by writing the minimal model that distinguishes your mechanism from alternatives.

### Critical boundary

The risk is toy models that are too flexible. Good taste uses the model to rule things out.

### Practice exercise

Write one comparative static that would fail under a rival theory.

## Skill 4: Frame financial stability as a policy tradeoff

### Generalisable research taste

Analyze policy tools by their effects on credit, risk-taking, and macro outcomes rather than
treating stability as free.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Monetary Policy as Financial-Stability Regulation
- Macro-finance policy papers
- Credit market studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests and arguments weigh benefits of risk reduction against costs to credit or output.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames policy as an optimization under constraints and externalities.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this to macroprudential policy, monetary policy, or bank regulation.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that welfare weights and model assumptions drive policy conclusions.

### Practice exercise

Write the tradeoff behind one financial-stability policy.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
