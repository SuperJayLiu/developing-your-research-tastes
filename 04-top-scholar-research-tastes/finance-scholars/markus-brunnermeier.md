# Markus Brunnermeier

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Financial fragility is an endogenous amplification system created by liquidity, leverage, beliefs,
and constraints interacting over time.

## Skill 1: Model amplification, not just shocks

### Generalisable research taste

Ask how a small shock becomes a crisis through leverage, liquidity, margins, and feedback.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Market Liquidity and Funding Liquidity
- Deciphering the Liquidity and Credit Crunch
- Bubbles and Crashes

**What the papers tend to test**

The theory and evidence trace feedback loops between funding constraints, asset sales, prices, and
balance sheets.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction often starts with an observed crisis pattern and asks why the response is
nonlinear.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill in banking, asset markets, macro shocks, and supply chains.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that amplification mechanisms need timing and state variables, not only narrative
arrows.

### Practice exercise

Draw a feedback loop for one crisis mechanism and mark the state variable.

## Skill 2: Separate market liquidity from funding liquidity

### Generalisable research taste

Distinguish the ability to sell assets from the ability to finance positions, then study their
interaction.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Market Liquidity and Funding Liquidity
- Liquidity crunch papers
- Macro-finance work

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests/modeling show how funding constraints reduce market liquidity and falling liquidity
tightens funding.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames liquidity as a system of interacting constraints rather than one scalar.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this in asset pricing, banking, or corporate finance where balance sheets and market depth
interact.

### Critical boundary

The risk is loose liquidity language. Good taste defines the liquidity margin precisely.

### Practice exercise

Take one liquidity claim and specify whether it is market liquidity, funding liquidity, or both.

## Skill 3: Turn crisis narratives into state-variable models

### Generalisable research taste

Translate crisis stories into dynamic models with leverage, constraints, beliefs, or liquidity as
state variables.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Liquidity and credit crunch analysis
- I Theory of Money
- Systemic risk papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests and models identify variables that propagate shocks and predict fragility.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction converts an episode into a general mechanism.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when historical crisis accounts are rich but analytically loose.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is overfitting one crisis. The mechanism should travel across episodes.

### Practice exercise

Choose one crisis story and name the state variable that moves through it.

## Skill 4: Write macro-finance as mechanism first

### Generalisable research taste

Make complex macro-finance arguments legible by foregrounding the amplification mechanism early.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Market Liquidity and Funding Liquidity
- I Theory of Money
- Crisis papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The writing typically introduces the core feedback loop before formal details.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The abstract/introduction tells readers the mechanism, why it matters, and what the model/evidence
adds.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by writing a one-paragraph mechanism summary before equations.

### Critical boundary

The risk is mechanism overload. The best introduction limits the number of moving parts.

### Practice exercise

Write your macro-finance mechanism in four arrows.
