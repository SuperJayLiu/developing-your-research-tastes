# Andrei Shleifer

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Great finance research often turns a simple friction—limits to arbitrage, legal institutions,
agency, or sentiment—into broad implications for markets and growth.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Skill 1: Turn behavioral frictions into market equilibrium

### Generalisable research taste

Do not stop at investor bias; ask why rational arbitrage does not eliminate it.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- The Limits of Arbitrage
- Investor Sentiment in the Stock Market
- Inefficient Markets

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests link sentiment, mispricing, constraints, and arbitrage costs to market prices.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames behavioral finance as an equilibrium problem, not a psychology anecdote.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill whenever a mispricing story needs a market-level persistence mechanism.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that limits-to-arbitrage explanations can become unfalsifiable without measures of
constraints.

### Practice exercise

Pick one anomaly and identify the arbitrage trade, risk, horizon, and constraint.

## Skill 2: Use simple models to organize messy institutions

### Generalisable research taste

Reduce legal, governance, or political complexity to a core economic friction while preserving
institutional relevance.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Law and Finance
- Corporate governance survey
- Transition-economy papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests often use cross-country legal variation, ownership structures, or governance mechanisms.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction names a simple institutional channel that can explain broad outcomes.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by finding the one contractual or legal constraint that drives the setting.

### Critical boundary

The risk is institutional oversimplification. A good paper knows which details matter and which can
be abstracted.

### Practice exercise

Write the core friction in one sentence and three institutional details it abstracts from.

## Skill 3: Explain why correction forces fail

### Generalisable research taste

When markets or institutions produce bad outcomes, identify why competition, arbitrage, entry, or
governance fails to fix them.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Limits of Arbitrage
- Corporate governance studies
- Law and finance work

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests look for barriers to correction: risk, coordination, law, agency, information, or
incentives.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction often moves from 'this should not persist' to 'here is why it persists.'

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill in markets, firms, politics, and organizations.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that every failure story needs a counterforce. Good taste quantifies or documents
why it is weak.

### Practice exercise

For any persistent inefficiency, list the correction force and why it is blocked.

## Skill 4: Write bold claims with a clear friction

### Generalisable research taste

Make ambitious arguments credible by centering them on a specific mechanism.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Law and Finance
- Limits of Arbitrage
- Investor sentiment papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The empirical and theoretical work often supports large claims through a simple organizing friction.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction style is direct: a field-level puzzle, a friction, and evidence across settings.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by removing secondary mechanisms until the core claim is visible.

### Critical boundary

The risk is excessive breadth. Boldness must be matched with scope conditions.

### Practice exercise

Write your paper's boldest claim and the single friction that supports it.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
