## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Skill 1: Make research infrastructure reusable

### Generalisable research taste

Turn data definitions, factor construction, and portfolio returns into shared infrastructure for the
field.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Kenneth French Data Library
- Common Risk Factors
- Five-Factor Asset Pricing Model

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests rely on transparent portfolio construction and repeated use across samples, markets, and
questions.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction often highlights that the model or data organize many existing findings.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill by making your constructed data or code useful beyond one paper.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that infrastructure choices embed assumptions. Users need documentation and
alternatives.

### Practice exercise

Document one constructed variable so another researcher could use it without asking you.

## Skill 2: Define factors by economic sorts, not black boxes

### Generalisable research taste

Construct factors from characteristics with interpretable economic meaning rather than purely
statistical extraction.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Three-factor model
- Five-factor model
- Size and value portfolio papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests examine whether portfolios based on size, value, profitability, or investment explain
returns.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing connects each factor to an economic candidate mechanism while remaining empirical.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by making each latent factor explainable in one economic sentence.

### Critical boundary

The risk is that interpretable labels may hide multiple mechanisms.

### Practice exercise

Take one factor and list at least two theories that could generate it.

## Skill 3: Use simple replication as a field standard

### Generalisable research taste

Make empirical design simple enough that anomalies and models can be replicated and compared.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- French Data Library
- Factor model papers
- Portfolio sort papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests use standard definitions and public data to lower the cost of scrutiny.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames contribution through comparability and breadth rather than novelty alone.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill by choosing definitions that others can reproduce easily unless complexity is
essential.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that standardization can suppress context-specific mechanisms.

### Practice exercise

Rewrite a complex empirical design into a minimal replicable version.

## Skill 4: Separate factor construction from factor interpretation

### Generalisable research taste

Distinguish the mechanical return factor from the theory that explains why it earns a premium.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Common Risk Factors
- Five-Factor Model
- Cross-sectional returns papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests can show explanatory power without fully settling risk versus mispricing interpretation.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing often presents the factor first as an empirical object, then discusses interpretation.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by naming which part of your result is construction and which part is explanation.

### Critical boundary

The critical boundary is overinterpretation: a factor is not automatically a structural mechanism.

### Practice exercise

Write two paragraphs for one factor: how it is built and what it might mean.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
