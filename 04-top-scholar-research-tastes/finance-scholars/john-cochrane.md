# John Cochrane

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Good asset pricing turns discount rates, cash flows, and risk premia into a unified language for
connecting theory and evidence.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Signature Settings

John Cochrane's work is useful here as a training example because the papers repeatedly connect substantive questions to concrete tests, models, measures, or institutional variation.

## Signature Methods

The relevant method is not copied mechanically. For each skill, the method is treated as a judgment device: it shows what kind of evidence the scholar considered persuasive.

## Skill 1: Decompose prices before interpreting them

### Generalisable research taste

Separate price movements into cash-flow news, discount-rate news, and risk-premium variation before
telling a story.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Discount Rates
- Return predictability papers
- Asset Pricing work

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests use return decompositions, predictability, and accounting identities to identify what part
of prices moves.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction often frames asset pricing as an interpretive problem: what economic object is
varying?

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill in any valuation setting: equities, bonds, housing, currencies, or firms.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that decompositions depend on assumptions and measurement horizons.

### Practice exercise

Take one price change and list cash-flow, discount-rate, and risk-premium explanations.

## Skill 2: Make accounting identities do conceptual work

### Generalisable research taste

Use identities and present-value logic to constrain stories about prices and returns.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Discount Rates
- Asset Pricing
- Return decomposition papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The theoretical tests show that certain verbal explanations imply measurable movements in returns,
dividends, or discount rates.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing uses simple equations to discipline intuition rather than to display technique.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by writing the identity your interpretation must satisfy.

### Critical boundary

The risk is mistaking identity for causality. Identities organize explanations; they do not identify
mechanisms alone.

### Practice exercise

Write the identity behind one empirical result in your project.

## Skill 3: Connect finance models to economic mechanisms

### Generalisable research taste

Do not stop at factor fit; ask what preferences, technologies, constraints, or beliefs generate the
pricing kernel.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Production-Based Asset Pricing
- Asset Pricing
- Macro-finance essays

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests link asset returns to production, consumption, investment, or macro states.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames a finance anomaly as an economic mechanism problem.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when your empirical factor needs a structural interpretation.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is overstructuring: a beautiful model can be too stylized for the data.

### Practice exercise

Choose one factor and propose one macro or firm-side mechanism behind it.

## Skill 4: Write theory as clarifying discipline

### Generalisable research taste

Use theory to remove ambiguity in empirical interpretation, not to obscure simple intuition.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Asset Pricing
- Discount Rates
- Macro-finance writing

**What the papers tend to test**

The work often begins from a puzzle and uses equations to show which explanations are logically
distinct.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction makes the reader care about the conceptual distinction before formalization.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by writing the intuition first, then the minimal equation that makes it sharp.

### Critical boundary

The risk is polemical clarity that underweights alternative models. Good taste keeps rival
mechanisms alive.

### Practice exercise

Write two competing explanations for the same return pattern and the test that separates them.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
