# Eugene Fama

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Finance theory becomes testable when prices, returns, and portfolios are used as disciplined
evidence about information, risk, and expected returns.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Skill 1: Turn market efficiency into a joint test

### Generalisable research taste

Frame efficiency claims as tests of both information processing and the asset-pricing model used to
define abnormal returns.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Efficient Capital Markets
- Market Efficiency, Long-Term Returns, and Behavioral Finance
- Event-study work

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests ask whether prices reflect information conditional on a benchmark model of expected
returns.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction usually clarifies the benchmark before interpreting deviations as anomalies.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill whenever you call something mispricing: state the model relative to which it is
mispriced.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is the joint-hypothesis problem; evidence against efficiency may be evidence against
the model.

### Practice exercise

Pick one anomaly and write the benchmark model it violates.

## Skill 2: Use portfolios to reveal systematic patterns

### Generalisable research taste

Sort assets into portfolios to make return patterns visible, robust, and economically interpretable.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- The Cross-Section of Expected Stock Returns
- Common Risk Factors
- Factor-model papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests compare average returns across size, value, market, or other characteristic-sorted
portfolios.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames characteristics as candidates for systematic risk or pricing errors.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by constructing portfolios that express the mechanism rather than only maximizing
t-statistics.

### Critical boundary

The risk is data mining. Good taste requires out-of-sample logic, economic motivation, and
robustness.

### Practice exercise

Design a portfolio sort for one hypothesized return mechanism and state the predicted pattern.

## Skill 3: Build benchmark models that organize debate

### Generalisable research taste

Create simple empirical models that become reference points for evaluating new anomalies and
explanations.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Common Risk Factors
- Fama-French three-factor and five-factor papers
- Cross-section papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests propose factors that explain broad return variation and then evaluate remaining anomalies.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing is often spare and cumulative: here is the empirical regularity, here is the benchmark,
here is what it explains.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill in any field by building a baseline model others can use to judge new claims.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is benchmark lock-in: useful models can become rituals detached from theory.

### Practice exercise

Write the minimal benchmark model your field should beat.

## Skill 4: Write with evidentiary restraint

### Generalisable research taste

Use precise language about what return evidence can and cannot prove.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Efficient Capital Markets
- Long-term returns and behavioral finance
- Factor papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The empirical tests are interpreted cautiously because alternative risk, model, and sampling
explanations remain possible.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction often avoids dramatic causal claims and instead defines the empirical test clearly.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this when evidence is strong but interpretation is contested.

### Critical boundary

The risk is excessive conservatism that blocks new mechanisms. Restraint should not become refusal
to theorize.

### Practice exercise

Rewrite an anomaly claim using three possible interpretations: risk, mispricing, and measurement.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
