# Robert Shiller

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Prices are not only discounted cash flows; they are also empirical traces of expectations,
narratives, volatility, and social dynamics.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Signature Methods

The relevant method is not copied mechanically. For each skill, the method is treated as a judgment device: it shows what kind of evidence the scholar considered persuasive.

## Skill 1: Use prices to test expectations

### Generalisable research taste

Ask whether observed prices can be reconciled with subsequent fundamentals and reasonable
expectation formation.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Do Stock Prices Move Too Much
- Excess volatility studies
- Irrational Exuberance

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests compare price movements with dividends, fundamentals, and variance bounds or expectations
data.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction sets up a tension between standard valuation logic and observed market movement.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill in asset markets, housing, crypto, or macro expectations.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is model dependence: 'too much' volatility depends on the benchmark and data horizon.

### Practice exercise

Choose one price series and name the fundamentals that should discipline it.

## Skill 2: Treat narratives as economic forces

### Generalisable research taste

Study stories, popular explanations, and social contagion as variables that influence expectations
and behavior.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Stock Prices and Social Dynamics
- Narrative Economics
- Irrational Exuberance

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests use historical narratives, survey evidence, media traces, and market behavior to connect
stories to outcomes.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing often begins with a market episode that standard models under-explain, then introduces
narrative as a mechanism.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this to bubbles, crises, adoption, policy debates, and investor behavior.

### Critical boundary

The risk is unfalsifiable storytelling. Good narrative economics needs measurable narrative proxies
and alternative mechanisms.

### Practice exercise

Pick one boom and write what narrative data would distinguish your story from a risk-based
explanation.

## Skill 3: Challenge benchmarks with simple contradictions

### Generalisable research taste

Use a simple empirical contradiction to force reconsideration of a dominant model.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Do Stock Prices Move Too Much
- Long-run valuation studies
- Survey expectation work

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests are often conceptually simple even if technically debated: compare model-implied variation
to observed variation.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction makes the reader see why the contradiction matters for the whole framework.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when a large theory produces a clear implication that data visibly violate.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that simple contradictions can be resolved by adding risks, frictions, or
expectations heterogeneity.

### Practice exercise

Find one benchmark implication and one fact that would make it uncomfortable.

## Skill 4: Write for audience expansion without losing rigor

### Generalisable research taste

Make finance research understandable to wider audiences while keeping the empirical puzzle
disciplined.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Irrational Exuberance
- Narrative Economics
- Survey and valuation papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The evidence combines formal tests, historical context, and accessible prose.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction often tells readers why a market fact matters beyond specialists.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by writing the first page so a non-finance economist understands the stakes.

### Critical boundary

The danger is that accessibility can invite overbroad claims. Strong writing keeps the testable
object clear.

### Practice exercise

Rewrite a technical finance puzzle for a general economics audience in 150 words.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
