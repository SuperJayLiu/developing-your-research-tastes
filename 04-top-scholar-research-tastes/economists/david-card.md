# David Card

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Good empirical work can overturn accepted beliefs by using transparent natural experiments and
taking comparison groups seriously.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Signature Settings

David Card's work is useful here as a training example because the papers repeatedly connect substantive questions to concrete tests, models, measures, or institutional variation.

## Signature Methods

The relevant method is not copied mechanically. For each skill, the method is treated as a judgment device: it shows what kind of evidence the scholar considered persuasive.

## Skill 1: Use simple natural experiments to test big claims

### Generalisable research taste

Take a widely believed relationship and test it in a setting where the comparison is unusually
transparent.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Minimum Wages and Employment
- The Impact of the Mariel Boatlift
- College proximity and schooling

**What the papers tend to test**

The empirical tests use policy changes, migration shocks, or geographic variation to confront
standard predictions.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction often sets up a conflict between conventional theory or belief and a concrete
empirical setting.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when a policy debate rests on assumed signs or magnitudes that can be tested in a
real shock.

### Critical boundary

The critical boundary is local context. A clean natural experiment may not settle every version of
the broader debate.

### Practice exercise

Choose one accepted belief in your field and find a setting that could make it testable.

## Skill 2: Privilege transparency over cleverness

### Generalisable research taste

Use designs and tables that readers can understand, replicate, and argue with rather than relying on
opaque sophistication.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Minimum wage case studies
- Mariel Boatlift
- Education returns papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests often make the comparison visible before adding econometric refinements.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing is direct: explain the event, comparison group, outcome, and potential confounds.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by making your first empirical table the simplest version of the design.

### Critical boundary

The risk is that simple comparisons can hide unobserved differences. Transparency must be paired
with threat analysis.

### Practice exercise

Create a one-figure version of your identification strategy.

## Skill 3: Take labor-market adjustment seriously

### Generalisable research taste

Do not assume a policy or shock maps mechanically into employment or wages; study adjustment margins
and institutions.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Minimum Wages and Employment
- Immigration and Inequality
- Labor-market studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests consider wages, employment, composition, substitutability, and local market structure.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames labor markets as empirical objects where theory offers predictions but not
automatic answers.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill in any market with frictions, adjustment costs, or institutional wage-setting.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is measurement: weak data can miss margins where adjustment occurs.

### Practice exercise

List five adjustment margins for a policy shock before estimating the average effect.

## Skill 4: Write controversial results with design humility

### Generalisable research taste

Challenge conventional wisdom while being explicit about the setting, comparison, and limits.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Minimum wage studies
- Mariel Boatlift
- Education returns studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The empirical evidence is strong because the paper does not overstate universality; it shows what a
particular shock reveals.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction usually motivates importance without claiming to end the debate.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this when your result is surprising: state the exact context in which it is surprising.

### Critical boundary

The risk is that humility can be misread as weakness. The contribution should be precise, not timid.

### Practice exercise

Write a surprising-result paragraph with one strong claim and two explicit limits.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
