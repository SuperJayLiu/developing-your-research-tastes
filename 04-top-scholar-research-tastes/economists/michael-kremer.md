# Michael Kremer

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Research can make progress by turning practical bottlenecks into clean tests of incentives,
externalities, and scalable policy design.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Signature Settings

Michael Kremer's work is useful here as a training example because the papers repeatedly connect substantive questions to concrete tests, models, measures, or institutional variation.

## Signature Methods

The relevant method is not copied mechanically. For each skill, the method is treated as a judgment device: it shows what kind of evidence the scholar considered persuasive.

## Skill 1: Find the externality in the policy problem

### Generalisable research taste

Look for spillovers, complementarities, or coordination failures that make private decisions differ
from social value.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Worms
- The O-Ring Theory of Economic Development
- Strong Medicine

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests often measure not only direct effects but also spillovers, treatment externalities, and
complementarities.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing pattern frames a practical problem as theoretically interesting because private
incentives miss social effects.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill in health, education, technology adoption, and finance whenever one actor's choice
affects others' returns.

### Critical boundary

The risk is missing equilibrium responses or measuring only nearby spillovers. Good taste states the
relevant spillover radius.

### Practice exercise

Pick one intervention and define who might benefit even if untreated.

## Skill 2: Turn bottlenecks into experimental design

### Generalisable research taste

Use a concrete delivery problem as the source of variation and learning.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Deworming studies
- Education interventions
- Vaccine-market design work

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests are built around program constraints, implementation timing, and randomized or quasi-
random access.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction typically starts with a high-stakes bottleneck and then explains the design that
can evaluate it.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by asking what prevents a useful policy from reaching people and whether that bottleneck
can be experimentally relaxed.

### Critical boundary

The limitation is that bottlenecks can be context-specific. Generalization requires identifying the
underlying friction.

### Practice exercise

Write a bottleneck-to-design table for a policy you know.

## Skill 3: Model complementarity as a development force

### Generalisable research taste

Treat poor outcomes as the result of weak links and complementarities rather than isolated deficits.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- The O-Ring Theory of Economic Development
- Development intervention work
- Education and health studies

**What the papers tend to test**

Theoretical tests ask whether small failures can have large aggregate consequences when tasks or
inputs are complementary.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing move is to show why a familiar development problem is not additive but multiplicative.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill in teams, supply chains, education, health, and financial systems.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that complementarity stories can explain too much. A strong paper identifies where
the weakest link is and how to test it.

### Practice exercise

Choose a production process and identify the input whose failure would lower returns to all others.

## Skill 4: Design for scalability early

### Generalisable research taste

Evaluate not only whether an intervention works under researcher control, but whether incentives,
markets, and institutions can scale it.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Strong Medicine
- Development RCTs
- Policy design papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests often consider take-up, costs, market creation, and implementation constraints.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames the contribution as a policy design problem, not simply a local effect
estimate.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by adding a scalability paragraph to every intervention paper.

### Critical boundary

The risk is premature scaling: external validity should be earned through mechanisms, cost data, and
institutional analysis.

### Practice exercise

Write a 'what would fail at scale?' section for one successful intervention.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
