# Joshua Angrist

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Credible causal research often comes from explaining exactly what variation identifies exactly which
effect, without hiding behind technical language.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Skill 1: State the comparison before the estimator

### Generalisable research taste

Before presenting an estimator, define the treated and comparison groups, why they are comparable,
and what causal contrast is actually learned.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Does Compulsory School Attendance Affect Schooling and Earnings?
- The Effect of Vietnam-Era Military Service on Earnings
- Using Maimonides' Rule

**What the papers tend to test**

The empirical tests are often simple once the comparison is clear: institutional rules or lotteries
generate variation, and the paper asks what causal contrast that variation identifies.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The abstract/introduction style tends to foreground the quasi-experiment and the estimand before
moving into econometrics.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill whenever you are tempted to describe a method first. Instead, write the comparison in
plain English before naming IV, RD, DiD, or matching.

### Critical boundary

This skill fails when the plain-English comparison is persuasive but the exclusion restriction or
sorting problem is not. Clarity is not the same as validity.

### Practice exercise

Write one sentence beginning: 'This design compares...' and one sentence beginning: 'This comparison
would fail if...'

## Skill 2: Use instruments as research designs, not buttons

### Generalisable research taste

Treat an instrument as a claim about institutional assignment and behavioral response, not merely as
a variable inserted into 2SLS.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Does Compulsory School Attendance Affect Schooling and Earnings?
- Identification and Estimation of Local Average Treatment Effects
- Vietnam draft lottery studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests ask whether the instrument changes the endogenous variable and whether the variation can
plausibly affect outcomes only through that channel for the relevant compliers.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing move is to explain who is moved by the instrument and therefore what population the
estimate represents.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by identifying the compliers, first stage, exclusion story, and possible direct effects
before estimating anything.

### Critical boundary

The critical boundary is external validity: IV often gives a local effect. The page should not
pretend that the LATE is the average effect for everyone.

### Practice exercise

For any IV idea, write the complier group in concrete human terms.

## Skill 3: Make econometrics readable enough to audit

### Generalisable research taste

Use transparent language, examples, and diagnostic questions so readers can audit the causal claim
instead of merely trusting notation.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Mostly Harmless Econometrics
- Mastering 'Metrics
- Identification and Estimation of Local Average Treatment Effects

**What the papers tend to test**

The method contribution is not only technical; it turns causal inference into a set of questions
about assignment, compliance, and interpretation.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introductions often translate technical disputes into the practical problem: what can we learn
from this variation?

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when teaching, writing methods sections, or presenting research designs. Replace one
formula with a sentence that states the identifying assumption.

### Critical boundary

The danger is oversimplification. Good taste simplifies the design without hiding fragile
assumptions.

### Practice exercise

Take one methods paragraph and rewrite it for a skeptical applied reader.

## Skill 4: Separate estimand from interpretation

### Generalisable research taste

First say what the design estimates; only then discuss whether that estimate answers the broader
policy or theory question.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Identification and Estimation of Local Average Treatment Effects
- Education return studies
- Class size studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests often make a sharp distinction between the parameter identified by variation and the
broader question readers may care about.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction usually avoids overclaiming by tying the contribution to a specific causal
contrast.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill by adding a 'what this estimate is and is not' paragraph to every empirical project.

### Critical boundary

The limitation is that readers may still want the broader answer. A tasteful paper tells them what
additional assumptions would be needed.

### Practice exercise

Write a paragraph titled 'What this design cannot tell us' for your current project.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
