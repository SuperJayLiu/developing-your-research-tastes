# Claudia Goldin

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Long-run economic change becomes understandable when careful measurement, institutions, and
narrative are combined into a coherent mechanism.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Skill 1: Use history to explain current frictions

### Generalisable research taste

Treat current inequality or labor-market patterns as the result of accumulated institutional and
technological changes.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- The Quiet Revolution
- A Grand Gender Convergence
- Career and Family

**What the papers tend to test**

The evidence combines long-run data, cohort patterns, institutional changes, and changes in
household or workplace constraints.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction often begins with a contemporary gap, then shows why the answer requires historical
sequencing.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when a cross-section looks puzzling because the relevant mechanism unfolded over
decades.

### Critical boundary

The danger is narrative determinism. Historical sequencing should generate testable implications
across cohorts, places, or institutions.

### Practice exercise

Take one current labor-market gap and write a timeline of mechanisms that could have produced it.

## Skill 2: Turn description into mechanism

### Generalisable research taste

Use descriptive patterns not as the final result but as clues that identify the relevant economic
mechanism.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- The Quiet Revolution
- A Grand Gender Convergence
- The Power of the Pill

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests examine cohort changes, timing, occupational structure, education, marriage, and workplace
flexibility.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The abstract/introduction typically gives readers a striking descriptive pattern and then asks what
mechanism can explain it.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by asking of every descriptive figure: what mechanism would make this shape appear?

### Critical boundary

The limitation is that rich description can be mistaken for causality. Good taste separates pattern,
mechanism, and identification.

### Practice exercise

Choose one figure and write three possible mechanisms behind its shape.

## Skill 3: Make measurement part of the contribution

### Generalisable research taste

Build or reinterpret long-run measures so the field can see a problem that was previously invisible
or mismeasured.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Historical labor-force studies
- Gender-gap studies
- Career and Family

**What the papers tend to test**

The empirical contribution often comes from data construction, cohort harmonization, and measurement
of work, education, occupation, and family constraints.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction explains why existing measures hide the real phenomenon.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when the right variable does not exist in a clean dataset.

### Critical boundary

The critical boundary is comparability: historical measures can change meaning across time.

### Practice exercise

Write a measurement memo for one variable that is easy to name but hard to measure.

## Skill 4: Write with narrative discipline

### Generalisable research taste

Use narrative to organize evidence, but keep the argument disciplined by chronology, measurement,
and economic mechanism.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- The Quiet Revolution
- Career and Family
- A Grand Gender Convergence

**What the papers tend to test**

The papers often move from historical fact to mechanism to implication, avoiding both pure
storytelling and pure regression output.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction reads like a guided puzzle: here is the change, here is why the usual explanation
is incomplete, here is the mechanism.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by outlining your paper as a sequence of facts that each force the next analytical step.

### Critical boundary

The danger is elegance that smooths over ambiguity. The paper should preserve uncertainty where
evidence is incomplete.

### Practice exercise

Write a three-act introduction: puzzle, missing mechanism, evidence strategy.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
