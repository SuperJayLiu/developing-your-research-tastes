# Jean Tirole

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Theory has good taste when it isolates the incentive or information constraint that makes
institutions, contracts, or regulation necessary.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Signature Settings

Jean Tirole's work is useful here as a training example because the papers repeatedly connect substantive questions to concrete tests, models, measures, or institutional variation.

## Signature Methods

The relevant method is not copied mechanically. For each skill, the method is treated as a judgment device: it shows what kind of evidence the scholar considered persuasive.

## Skill 1: Isolate the incentive constraint

### Generalisable research taste

Find the hidden information, hidden action, or commitment problem that drives the institutional
design.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Incentives in Procurement and Regulation
- Contract theory papers
- Corporate finance theory work

**What the papers tend to test**

The theory tests alternative contracts or regulations by whether they satisfy incentive
compatibility.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction often starts from an institutional puzzle and identifies the friction requiring
design.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill in regulation, corporate finance, platforms, and organizations.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is realism: the key constraint must be plausible in the actual institution.

### Practice exercise

For one institution, write the incentive constraint that any solution must satisfy.

## Skill 2: Use theory to design policy, not just explain

### Generalisable research taste

Build models that compare institutional arrangements and derive design implications.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Regulation theory work
- Industrial organization models
- Corporate finance theory

**What the papers tend to test**

The theoretical tests compare contracts, regulatory rules, or market structures under explicit
constraints.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames the model as a way to reason about policy tradeoffs.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this when institutions can be redesigned, not merely observed.

### Critical boundary

The risk is policy prescriptions sensitive to assumptions.

### Practice exercise

Write two policies and the model condition under which each is better.

## Skill 3: Turn complex industries into strategic games

### Generalisable research taste

Reduce industry detail to players, information, incentives, and strategic interactions.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Industrial organization work
- Two-sided market papers
- Regulation models

**What the papers tend to test**

The theory tests equilibrium behavior under different rules or information structures.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction makes the complex industry legible through the core strategic conflict.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill for platforms, banking, energy, healthcare, or digital markets.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is omitting institutional details that change payoffs or constraints.

### Practice exercise

Write the players, actions, payoffs, and information in one market.

## Skill 4: Write theory introductions around a real institution

### Generalisable research taste

Motivate formal theory with an institution whose design cannot be understood without the model's
friction.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Regulation work
- Corporate finance theory
- Platform theory

**What the papers tend to test**

The introduction links abstract constraints to real contracts, firms, regulators, or platforms.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The theory then gives a disciplined explanation rather than generic intuition.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by opening a theory paper with the institution before the equations.

### Critical boundary

The risk is decorative examples. The institution must need the theory.

### Practice exercise

Write an opening paragraph where a real institution reveals an incentive problem.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
