# Matthew Gentzkow

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Information, persuasion, and media markets become researchable when text, choices, and market
structure are converted into measurable economic objects.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Signature Settings

Matthew Gentzkow's work is useful here as a training example because the papers repeatedly connect substantive questions to concrete tests, models, measures, or institutional variation.

## Signature Methods

The relevant method is not copied mechanically. For each skill, the method is treated as a judgment device: it shows what kind of evidence the scholar considered persuasive.

## Skill 1: Measure informational objects directly

### Generalisable research taste

Turn slant, ideology, persuasion, attention, or narratives into variables that can be validated and
linked to behavior.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- What Drives Media Slant?
- Text as Data
- Ideological Segregation Online and Offline

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests use text, consumption data, or market outcomes to measure information environments and
then connect them to incentives.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction usually begins with an important but vague informational concept and promises a way
to measure it.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when your mechanism is informational but your data only indirectly observe
information.

### Critical boundary

The risk is measurement reification: a text score is not the concept itself. Validation is part of
the contribution.

### Practice exercise

Pick one vague concept and propose two validation tests for a text-based measure.

## Skill 2: Connect content to incentives

### Generalisable research taste

Explain media or communication choices as responses to demand, competition, reputation, or strategic
incentives.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- What Drives Media Slant?
- Media Bias and Reputation
- Media market studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The empirical/theoretical tests separate supply-side ideology from demand-side incentives and market
competition.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing frames content not as neutral description but as an equilibrium outcome.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this in any setting where organizations produce information: analysts, firms, media,
platforms, or central banks.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that incentives are often inferred indirectly. Good taste uses alternative
explanations and robustness.

### Practice exercise

Choose one information producer and list demand-side and supply-side incentives shaping its message.

## Skill 3: Use text as data with economic purpose

### Generalisable research taste

Use NLP and text methods only after defining the economic object and behavioral link the text is
supposed to capture.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Text as Data
- Media slant papers
- Political communication studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests validate text measures against human coding, known events, behavioral outcomes, or market
incentives.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction makes the text method serve a substantive puzzle, not the other way around.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this by writing a 'what does the text measure economically?' paragraph before modeling text.

### Critical boundary

The danger is topic-model tourism: impressive text analysis without an economic mechanism.

### Practice exercise

For a text dataset, state the economic mechanism in one sentence and the validation test in another.

## Skill 4: Write methodological humility into measurement papers

### Generalisable research taste

Show what a new measure can and cannot identify, and report sensitivity to choices that affect
interpretation.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Text as Data
- Sensitivity of parameter estimates papers
- Media measurement papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests examine sensitivity, alternative measures, and links between measurement choices and
substantive conclusions.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction often positions the method as enabling disciplined inference, not automatic truth.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by including a measurement appendix that makes subjective choices visible.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that too much sensitivity can obscure the main finding. Good taste prioritizes the
choices that matter.

### Practice exercise

List three researcher degrees of freedom in your measurement strategy.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
