# Nicholas Bloom

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Firm-level productivity puzzles become researchable when internal practices, management, and
organization are measured as economic variables.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Skill 1: Make hidden organizational practices measurable

### Generalisable research taste

Convert management, organization, and workplace practices into comparable measures that can explain
productivity variation.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Measuring and Explaining Management Practices
- Does Management Matter?
- Firm organization studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests use surveys, experiments, and cross-firm comparisons to link management practices to
outcomes.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames an important residual—productivity differences—as partly explainable by a
hard-to-measure organizational variable.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill whenever the likely mechanism is inside the firm or institution rather than in prices
alone.

### Critical boundary

The risk is survey measurement error and reverse causality. Good taste validates measures and seeks
exogenous variation.

### Practice exercise

Design three survey questions for a hidden organizational practice and one validation check.

## Skill 2: Use field experiments inside firms

### Generalisable research taste

Test management and productivity mechanisms by intervening within organizations rather than only
observing across them.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Does Management Matter?
- Management intervention studies
- Working-from-home studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The empirical tests use randomized or quasi-random changes in practices to estimate productivity and
behavior effects.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing emphasizes why the firm interior matters and how access permits a cleaner test.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by asking whether a firm policy can be randomized, phased in, or compared across units.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is external validity: one firm or country may not represent all organizations.

### Practice exercise

Write one within-organization experiment that could test a productivity mechanism.

## Skill 3: Treat management as technology

### Generalisable research taste

Analyze management practices as productive technologies that diffuse, vary in quality, and interact
with competition and skills.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Management practices across countries
- Does Management Matter?
- Firm productivity studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests look for variation in adoption, quality, diffusion, and effects on productivity.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction turns a soft concept into an economic input with measurable returns.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill in finance, public economics, education, or health organizations where internal
practices matter.

### Critical boundary

The danger is assuming best practices are universally optimal. Fit with local constraints matters.

### Practice exercise

Choose one management practice and state when it would have high and low returns.

## Skill 4: Write practical measurement as high-status research

### Generalisable research taste

Make readers see measurement infrastructure as central to answering a major economic question.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Management measurement papers
- WFH survey work
- Firm productivity studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The empirical contribution is partly the construction of standardized measures and repeated data
collection.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The abstract/introduction often states that the field could not answer a big question because it
lacked comparable measurement.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by justifying measurement work through the theoretical or policy question it unlocks.

### Critical boundary

The limitation is that measurement alone is not enough. The paper must show what new inference
becomes possible.

### Practice exercise

Write a paragraph explaining what question becomes answerable once your variable is measured.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
