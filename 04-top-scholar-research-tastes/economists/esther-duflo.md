# Esther Duflo

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Strong development research converts policy complexity into experimental learning while staying
close to implementation details and mechanisms.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Skill 1: Turn policy questions into learning systems

### Generalisable research taste

Design studies so each intervention teaches something about behavior, constraints, and
implementation, not only whether a program works.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Using Randomization in Development Economics Research
- The Miracle of Microfinance?
- A Multifaceted Program Causes Lasting Progress for the Very Poor

**What the papers tend to test**

The empirical tests often combine treatment effects with mechanisms, heterogeneity, take-up, and
implementation measures.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction typically motivates a real policy puzzle, then narrows it into a testable
intervention and learning objective.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when evaluating policy: specify the decision the study should inform and the
behavioral mechanism it should test.

### Critical boundary

The risk is program-specific learning that does not travel. Good taste requires explaining which
mechanism is portable and which implementation details are local.

### Practice exercise

Write a program-evaluation question as both 'does it work?' and 'what would we learn if it works or
fails?'

## Skill 2: Embed identification inside field reality

### Generalisable research taste

Build credible designs that respect how people, organizations, and governments actually implement
policies.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- School construction in Indonesia
- Field experiment papers
- Poverty intervention studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests use field variation or randomization, but they also track compliance, dosage, operational
constraints, and behavior.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing often explains the institutional setting early because the design is not credible
without it.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by treating implementation details as part of research design, not as background color.

### Critical boundary

The danger is losing clean inference when implementation is messy. The page should distinguish
design validity from operational complexity.

### Practice exercise

For one policy, list five implementation details that could change the estimated effect.

## Skill 3: Measure mechanisms, not just outcomes

### Generalisable research taste

Collect intermediate outcomes that reveal why an intervention works, who responds, and which
constraint is binding.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- The Miracle of Microfinance?
- Education and health field experiments
- Graduation program studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests examine take-up, investment, behavior, learning, and heterogeneity to distinguish
channels.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames the intervention as a test of a behavioral or economic constraint, not
merely as a treatment label.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill by adding mechanism measures before collecting data rather than after seeing results.

### Critical boundary

Mechanism measures can become a fishing expedition. Good taste pre-specifies the mechanism logic and
treats nulls seriously.

### Practice exercise

Write three mechanism variables you would collect for an intervention and why each matters.

## Skill 4: Write policy relevance without overclaiming

### Generalisable research taste

State why the problem matters for policy while being precise about setting, treatment, and external
validity.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Randomization in Development Economics
- Microfinance studies
- Poverty program studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The empirical evidence is often localized; the writing makes broader relevance through mechanism and
careful interpretation.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The abstract usually tells readers the policy issue, the intervention, the design, and the key
margin learned.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by writing policy implications as conditional lessons: if the same constraint binds, this
evidence is informative.

### Critical boundary

The limitation is that policymakers may want universal answers. Tasteful research resists universal
claims from one setting.

### Practice exercise

Rewrite a broad policy conclusion into a mechanism-based conditional statement.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
