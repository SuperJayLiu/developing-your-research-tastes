# Raj Chetty

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Administrative data can become a scientific instrument when it maps broad social questions into
granular, validated, policy-relevant facts.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Signature Settings

Raj Chetty's work is useful here as a training example because the papers repeatedly connect substantive questions to concrete tests, models, measures, or institutional variation.

## Signature Methods

The relevant method is not copied mechanically. For each skill, the method is treated as a judgment device: it shows what kind of evidence the scholar considered persuasive.

## Skill 1: Turn big data into public scientific infrastructure

### Generalisable research taste

Build datasets, maps, or measures that let many researchers and policymakers see a problem at useful
resolution.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- The Opportunity Atlas
- Where is the Land of Opportunity?
- Mobility studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests use linked administrative records to measure mobility across places, cohorts, and groups.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames the data not as a technical advantage but as a way to make an important
question visible.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when your data construction can create reusable measurement infrastructure.

### Critical boundary

The risk is that scale can distract from causality. A map is not a mechanism unless linked to
variation and tests.

### Practice exercise

Imagine one public map your field needs and list the raw records required to build it.

## Skill 2: Validate descriptive facts before selling causal stories

### Generalisable research taste

Treat large descriptive patterns as objects that need validation, robustness, and decomposition
before causal interpretation.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Mobility maps
- Neighborhood effects papers
- Tax salience papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests often compare across cohorts, siblings, movers, exposure duration, and alternative
definitions.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing pattern separates 'what the data show' from 'what may explain it.'

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by adding a validation section before the mechanism section.

### Critical boundary

The limitation is that validated description may still not answer the policy counterfactual.

### Practice exercise

Write the strongest descriptive fact in your project and two validation tests.

## Skill 3: Use exposure gradients to learn mechanisms

### Generalisable research taste

Use variation in timing, age, or duration of exposure to distinguish place effects from selection.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- The Effects of Exposure to Better Neighborhoods on Children
- Neighborhood mobility studies
- Opportunity Atlas work

**What the papers tend to test**

The empirical tests ask whether outcomes vary with age at move or duration of exposure in ways
predicted by causal place effects.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction motivates not only whether neighborhoods matter but when and through what exposure
process they matter.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill in any setting where treatment exposure accumulates over time.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that timing may be endogenous. Good taste asks why people move when they do.

### Practice exercise

For one treatment, draw predicted effects by exposure duration under two mechanisms.

## Skill 4: Write for policy with measurement precision

### Generalisable research taste

Make policy relevance concrete by producing interpretable, localized, and validated measures rather
than broad claims.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Opportunity Atlas
- Mobility studies
- Tax policy studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The evidence links a general policy question to specific margins: place, age, income rank, school,
or tax visibility.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The abstract/introduction usually states the policy problem, the new measurement, and the empirical
design in a compact way.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by translating a regression result into the policy unit that would act on it.

### Critical boundary

The risk is technocratic overconfidence: precise measures can still miss politics, implementation,
or general equilibrium.

### Practice exercise

Rewrite one result as guidance for a policymaker and add two cautions.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
