# Abhijit Banerjee

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Good development research often starts from a concrete puzzle in poor households or institutions and
builds theory from observed constraints rather than from idealized agents.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Skill 1: Build theory from micro details

### Generalisable research taste

Use granular observations about households, firms, or institutions to discipline which model is
worth writing.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- The Economic Lives of the Poor
- Poor Economics
- Remedying Education

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests often begin with behavior that standard models do not immediately explain, then test
constraints, beliefs, or frictions.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing style frequently starts with a concrete human or institutional puzzle before
generalizing.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by collecting descriptive facts before choosing the theoretical frame.

### Critical boundary

The risk is anecdotal theorizing. The detail must generate testable mechanisms, not just colorful
motivation.

### Practice exercise

Take one descriptive fact and write two competing models that could explain it.

## Skill 2: Treat low take-up as information

### Generalisable research taste

When people do not adopt useful products or programs, treat the failure as evidence about
constraints, beliefs, trust, or costs.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- The Miracle of Microfinance?
- Health and education adoption studies
- Poverty intervention studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests examine take-up, usage, behavior after access, and heterogeneity rather than only average
treatment effects.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames non-adoption as a puzzle rather than as noise.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill in any setting where a seemingly beneficial innovation is underused.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that low take-up can come from mundane design failures. Good taste separates
mechanism from implementation defect.

### Practice exercise

Choose one low-adoption technology and list four mechanisms that could explain non-use.

## Skill 3: Convert policy complexity into behavioral mechanisms

### Generalisable research taste

Translate a messy policy environment into a small set of frictions: information, incentives,
attention, liquidity, risk, or trust.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Poor Economics
- Remedying Education
- Graduation program studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests usually distinguish which constraint is binding, often through interventions that relax
one friction at a time.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing move is to make the policy problem analytically simple without making the setting
unrealistic.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by reducing a program to a mechanism diagram with inputs, constraints, behaviors, and
outcomes.

### Critical boundary

The risk is choosing the mechanism after seeing the result. Pre-analysis or transparent theory helps
discipline this move.

### Practice exercise

Draw a mechanism diagram for one policy and mark where evidence is needed.

## Skill 4: Write accessible introductions with analytical bite

### Generalisable research taste

Make a paper readable by beginning with a concrete question, but preserve rigor by quickly
identifying the mechanism and evidence.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- The Economic Lives of the Poor
- Poor Economics-related papers
- Field experiment papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The introduction/abstract style often avoids unnecessary jargon while still moving toward a testable
claim.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The empirical work then supports the accessible story with design, measurement, and mechanisms.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill by writing the first paragraph for an intelligent non-specialist and the second for a
skeptical economist.

### Critical boundary

The danger is popular clarity without identification clarity. The introduction must not substitute
vividness for evidence.

### Practice exercise

Write two opening paragraphs: one motivating the puzzle, one stating the research design.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
