# Susan Athey

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Use modern computation to sharpen causal, policy, and market-design questions rather than to replace
economic reasoning.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Skill 1: Use machine learning to target economic questions

### Generalisable research taste

Use prediction and flexible models to improve heterogeneity analysis, policy learning, or
measurement while keeping the economic estimand explicit.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Machine Learning Methods That Economists Should Know About
- Recursive Partitioning for Heterogeneous Causal Effects
- Policy Learning papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests combine predictive tools with causal objects such as treatment effects, counterfactual
policies, or heterogeneous responses.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction usually explains why off-the-shelf prediction is insufficient without causal
structure.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when ML can help discover heterogeneity or improve decisions, but define the economic
object first.

### Critical boundary

The danger is treating predictive accuracy as causal insight. Good taste keeps a wall between
prediction tasks and causal claims.

### Practice exercise

Take one ML idea and state the causal estimand it helps estimate or the policy decision it helps
choose.

## Skill 2: Turn platform design into testable economics

### Generalisable research taste

Translate digital-market design choices into hypotheses about incentives, matching, information, and
welfare.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Platform market papers
- Auction and marketplace design work
- Digital economy studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests use platform variation, experiments, or structural models to assess behavior under
alternative rules.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames a platform feature as an economic mechanism, not merely a business detail.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this to any digital platform: define participants, frictions, rule changes, and welfare
margins.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is data access and platform endogeneity. Platform changes are rarely random without
careful design.

### Practice exercise

Choose one app feature and write the incentive problem it solves or creates.

## Skill 3: Combine method innovation with applied payoff

### Generalisable research taste

Develop methods in response to applied problems where existing tools cannot answer the decision
question well.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Causal forests / heterogeneous effects work
- Policy learning work
- Economics and ML surveys

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests evaluate whether the method improves estimation or decisions in settings economists care
about.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing pattern starts with an applied challenge and then justifies the method as the needed
tool.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when writing technical work: state the applied decision failure that motivates the
method.

### Critical boundary

The risk is method-first writing where the application is decorative. Good taste demands a real
applied constraint.

### Practice exercise

Write the first paragraph of a methods paper without mentioning the method name.

## Skill 4: Audit algorithmic taste with economic structure

### Generalisable research taste

Ask whether an algorithmic recommendation respects incentives, equilibrium, fairness, and policy
constraints.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Policy learning papers
- Platform design papers
- ML for economics writing

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests go beyond fit to consider treatment assignment, decision rules, welfare criteria, and
unintended behavior.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction often emphasizes that better decisions require both data and economic objectives.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill in AI-for-research projects: every AI suggestion should be checked against the
economic object and decision context.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is that some objectives are normative. The paper should state whose welfare or loss
function is optimized.

### Practice exercise

Write the objective function behind one algorithmic recommendation you use.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
