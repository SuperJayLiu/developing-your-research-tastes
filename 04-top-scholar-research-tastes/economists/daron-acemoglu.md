# Daron Acemoglu

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Big questions become researchable when institutions, technology, or politics are converted into
explicit mechanisms and testable variation.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Signature Settings

Daron Acemoglu's work is useful here as a training example because the papers repeatedly connect substantive questions to concrete tests, models, measures, or institutional variation.

## Signature Methods

The relevant method is not copied mechanically. For each skill, the method is treated as a judgment device: it shows what kind of evidence the scholar considered persuasive.

## Skill 1: Model institutions as causal systems

### Generalisable research taste

Treat institutions not as background context but as causal variables with channels, persistence, and
counterfactual implications.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- The Colonial Origins of Comparative Development
- Reversal of Fortune
- Democracy Does Cause Growth

**What the papers tend to test**

The empirical move is to link institutional variation to long-run outcomes and then look for
historically grounded sources of variation that can separate institutions from geography or
contemporaneous income.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The abstract/introduction pattern usually starts with a very large outcome difference, names the
missing institutional mechanism, and then promises a design that can make a big historical claim
testable.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when your topic has a large outcome gap but the causal object is vague. Define the
institution, specify the channel, then ask what historical or policy variation changes the
institution but not every other mechanism.

### Critical boundary

This taste can overreach if the institutional measure is too coarse or if the historical instrument
affects outcomes through many unmodeled channels. The skill is powerful only when mechanisms and
threats are stated explicitly.

### Practice exercise

Pick one persistent cross-region difference. Define one institution behind it, one channel, one
counterfactual, and two threats to identification.

## Skill 2: Make big questions empirically tractable

### Generalisable research taste

Start from a civilization-scale or economy-wide question, but reduce it to a proxy, treatment, model
object, or comparison that can be tested.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- The Colonial Origins of Comparative Development
- Robots and Jobs
- The Race between Man and Machine

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests typically convert broad ideas such as institutions, technology, democracy, or automation
into measurable exposure variables and then ask whether predicted comparative statics appear in
data.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing move is to keep the ambition high while quickly telling the reader what the paper can
actually observe, estimate, or model.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by forcing every broad research question to pass through a tractability filter: what is
the observable variation, what is the unit of analysis, and what prediction could fail?

### Critical boundary

The danger is pseudo-tractability: a narrow proxy can be mistaken for the whole concept. The
introduction should say what the proxy captures and what it cannot capture.

### Practice exercise

Rewrite one broad idea in your field into three empirically testable versions, each with a different
unit of analysis.

## Skill 3: Combine theory with historical or institutional evidence

### Generalisable research taste

Use a model to discipline the mechanism, then use historical or institutional variation to show the
mechanism has empirical bite.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Reversal of Fortune
- Why Nations Fail
- Directed Technical Change

**What the papers tend to test**

Theory supplies the mechanism and comparative statics; historical or empirical evidence checks
whether the mechanism helps explain observed variation rather than remaining a verbal story.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction often works by first naming a puzzle, then identifying a mechanism, then promising
evidence that the mechanism is not just plausible but consequential.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when a story feels persuasive but loose. Write the mechanism as a chain of incentives
and then ask what empirical pattern the chain predicts.

### Critical boundary

The risk is confirmation bias: theory can make a preferred historical narrative sound inevitable. A
strong page should list rival mechanisms and evidence that would weaken the claim.

### Practice exercise

Take a historical narrative you like and write three predictions that would be false if the
mechanism were wrong.

## Skill 4: Treat technology as distributional, not neutral

### Generalisable research taste

Study technology by asking whose tasks, wages, market power, and political incentives it changes,
rather than treating it as a neutral productivity shock.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Robots and Jobs
- The Race between Man and Machine
- Why Do New Technologies Complement Skills?

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests look for heterogeneous exposure, displacement, complementarity, and task-level mechanisms
rather than only average productivity effects.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing pattern is to frame technology as a force that reallocates economic opportunities, then
use theory or exposure designs to separate winners, losers, and margins of adjustment.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this to AI, automation, financial technology, or platforms: define who is substituted, who is
complemented, and what institution mediates the effect.

### Critical boundary

The boundary condition is that exposure measures may capture local demand shocks or industry trends.
Good taste requires mechanism tests, not only exposure regressions.

### Practice exercise

Choose one technology and write a two-column table: groups complemented by it and groups substituted
by it.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
