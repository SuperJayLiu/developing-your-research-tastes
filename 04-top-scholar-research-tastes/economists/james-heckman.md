# James Heckman

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Good empirical work treats selection, heterogeneity, and life-cycle dynamics as central objects
rather than nuisances to be controlled away.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Skill 1: Make selection the research object

### Generalisable research taste

Ask who enters the treatment, sample, program, or market, and what that selection reveals.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Sample Selection Bias as a Specification Error
- Selection model papers
- Program evaluation work

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests model the selection process and show how ignoring it biases interpretation.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames missing or selected observations as a substantive problem, not a technical
footnote.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill whenever participation, observability, or treatment uptake is non-random.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is model dependence. Selection corrections require strong assumptions and exclusion
logic.

### Practice exercise

Write the selection equation behind one dataset you use.

## Skill 2: Treat treatment effects as heterogeneous

### Generalisable research taste

Ask whose treatment effect is being estimated and why effects differ across people, margins, or life
stages.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Treatment-effect heterogeneity work
- Human capital evaluation papers
- Program evaluation methods

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests distinguish average, marginal, local, and policy-relevant treatment effects.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction avoids one-number thinking and ties effects to policy margins.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when programs affect heterogeneous populations.

### Critical boundary

The risk is heterogeneity fishing. Theory should guide which dimensions matter.

### Practice exercise

Write three groups for whom a treatment should have different effects and why.

## Skill 3: Connect skills formation to dynamic complementarity

### Generalisable research taste

Study human capital as a dynamic process where early investments affect later returns.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Early childhood and human capital papers
- Skill formation work
- Program evaluation studies

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests look for timing, persistence, complementarities, and long-run outcomes.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction frames early interventions as changing trajectories, not only short-run outcomes.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this in education, health, labor, and development.

### Critical boundary

The boundary is long-run confounding and attrition.

### Practice exercise

Draw a life-cycle diagram for one intervention.

## Skill 4: Write methodological critique as substantive warning

### Generalisable research taste

Use econometric critique to show how wrong policy conclusions arise, not merely to display technical
weakness.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Sample selection papers
- Treatment-effect interpretation papers
- Program evaluation critiques

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests show how alternative assumptions change estimated effects and policy interpretation.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction connects technical bias to real-world decision errors.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when writing methods or robustness sections.

### Critical boundary

The risk is critique without constructive alternative.

### Practice exercise

Write a paragraph explaining how one bias would change a policy decision.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
