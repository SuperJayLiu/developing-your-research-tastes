# Guido Imbens

## One-Sentence Research Taste

Good causal inference begins by clarifying the estimand, assignment mechanism, and assumptions
before computation or modeling ambition.

## Extraction Lens

The skills below are extracted by asking four questions across the scholar's papers:

1. **Question taste**: What kinds of questions does the scholar repeatedly choose?
2. **Design or theory taste**: What empirical design, model structure, or mechanism makes the question answerable?
3. **Testing taste**: What would the paper have to show for the claim to be believable?
4. **Writing taste**: How do the abstract and introduction make the contribution legible?

The goal is not to imitate the scholar's topic. The goal is to extract a transferable judgment rule.

## Signature Questions

- What hidden mechanism makes a large economic or financial fact understandable?
- What design, model, or measurement choice turns that mechanism into evidence?
- What alternative explanation must be ruled out before the claim becomes credible?

## Skill 1: Define the estimand before estimation

### Generalisable research taste

Specify the causal quantity of interest before choosing the estimator, covariates, or machine-
learning procedure.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Identification and Estimation of Local Average Treatment Effects
- The Econometrics of Randomized Experiments
- Matching methods and treatment-effect papers

**What the papers tend to test**

The theoretical tests clarify which assumptions identify which treatment-effect parameter,
separating average effects, local effects, and conditional effects.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing pattern is to make the object of inference explicit early, reducing ambiguity about what
the paper claims to learn.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by writing the estimand in words and notation before looking at data.

### Critical boundary

The risk is formal clarity without substantive relevance. An estimand can be clean but not important
for the question.

### Practice exercise

For a paper you read, write the estimand the authors actually identify and the estimand readers
might wish they had.

## Skill 2: Treat design and analysis as separate steps

### Generalisable research taste

Decide how credible variation is created or approximated before optimizing prediction, controls, or
standard errors.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Causal Inference for Statistics, Social, and Biomedical Sciences
- Matching and propensity score papers
- Randomized experiment econometrics

**What the papers tend to test**

The tests distinguish the assignment mechanism from the outcome model, so credibility does not rest
entirely on functional form.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The abstract/introduction typically emphasizes the design problem before discussing estimation
details.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill by writing a design memo that contains no results, only assignment, balance, threats,
and target population.

### Critical boundary

The limitation is that observational settings rarely provide clean designs. Good taste states how
much design credibility is constructed rather than given.

### Practice exercise

Before estimating, draft a one-page design memo and include three threats.

## Skill 3: Make assumptions visible and test-adjacent

### Generalisable research taste

State identifying assumptions in a way that suggests diagnostics, sensitivity checks, or
falsification tests.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- LATE papers
- Matching methods
- Randomized experiment econometrics

**What the papers tend to test**

The formal contribution maps assumptions to observable or partially observable implications, making
causal claims more auditable.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The writing style generally favors assumption-by-assumption exposition instead of burying
identification in algebra.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Apply this by converting every identifying assumption into one diagnostic or placebo question.

### Critical boundary

Some assumptions cannot be tested directly. The skill is to make them discussable, not to pretend
they are verified.

### Practice exercise

List three assumptions in your project and one observable pattern that would make each less
plausible.

## Skill 4: Use formalism to protect interpretation

### Generalisable research taste

Use notation to prevent verbal overclaiming, especially when treatment effects are heterogeneous or
assignment is imperfect.

### How this taste is extracted

**Evidence anchors**

- Identification and Estimation of Local Average Treatment Effects
- Treatment-effect heterogeneity papers
- Causal inference texts

**What the papers tend to test**

The theory shows how the same estimator can answer different questions under different assumptions
and populations.

**What the abstract/introduction tends to do**

The introduction often frames the contribution as preventing a common interpretive mistake rather
than simply proposing a new technique.

### Why this becomes a transferable skill

Use this skill when a result sounds simple but the population or margin is subtle.

### Critical boundary

The risk is that formalism becomes detached from applied stakes. The skill works best when notation
clarifies a real interpretive hazard.

### Practice exercise

Take one published estimate and identify the population for whom it is most credible.

## What Not to Overgeneralize

- Do not copy the scholar's topic and call it taste.
- Do not copy the method without the identification, mechanism, or modeling discipline that made it persuasive.
- Do not treat a famous paper as a universal recipe. Each skill has scope conditions.
- Do not infer too much from abstracts alone; the full introduction, design, tests, and limitations matter.
