# Evidence Anchors

Use this file to keep Nobel-derived taste claims tied to sources rather than reputation.

## Evidence Hierarchy

Start with official NobelPrize.org materials when the skill is about a laureate's recognized contribution. The local cache records official source metadata in `00-start-here/_support/evidence/nobel-open-access/download_manifest.json`, extracted text metadata in `00-start-here/_support/evidence/nobel-open-access/nobel_pdf_read_index.json`, and Nobel API data in `00-start-here/_support/evidence/nobel-open-access/nobel_api_eco.json`.

Use `04-top-scholar-research-tastes/nobel-laureates/nobel-taste-atlas.md` for the first-pass synthesis of laureate motivations, official PDF evidence, and portable taste patterns.

Use `04-top-scholar-research-tastes/nobel-laureates/nobel-skill-map.md` as an index only. It is not the final skill text.

Use `04-top-scholar-research-tastes/nobel-laureates/nobel-detailed-skill-cards.md` for reader-facing long-form cards.

Use author-hosted papers, working papers, journal pages, and uploaded PDFs only when the task requires paper-level detail. Do not bulk-copy paywalled papers into the repo.

## Anchor Patterns

For causal design and measurement discipline, look for evidence that the scholar repeatedly names the learning object, separates design from interpretation, or turns measurement limits into research structure.

For formal theory and allocation, look for evidence that the scholar builds a benchmark, adds a load-bearing departure, and derives a prediction, allocation result, or welfare comparison.

For growth, innovation, and historical change, look for evidence that the scholar turns a long-run pattern into a state variable, mechanism, comparison, or bounded lesson.

For institutions and governance, look for evidence that institutional detail changes incentives, information, bargaining, enforcement, participation, or feasible choices.

For incentives, information, contracts, and design, look for evidence that the scholar names the private object, binding constraint, benchmark failure, and design consequence.

For finance, macroeconomics, and market signals, look for evidence that prices or quantities are interpreted through benchmarks and competing mechanisms rather than treated as self-explanatory.

For welfare, behavior, poverty, and human decision-making, look for evidence that the scholar defines the decision problem, constraint, intervention, welfare object, and external-validity boundary.

For markets, trade, allocation, and design, look for evidence that the scholar treats markets as allocation systems shaped by frictions, rules, geography, search, or design.

## Source Discipline

If a card cannot name a source family, mark it provisional. If a card makes a claim about a specific scholar, cite the scholar page, Nobel atlas entry, official Nobel source, or paper evidence used. If a card draws from a broad tradition rather than one scholar, say so directly.

Do not claim that a laureate "always" uses a move. Say the move is a recurring pattern, a Nobel-linked tradition, or a portable lesson extracted from the evidence.
