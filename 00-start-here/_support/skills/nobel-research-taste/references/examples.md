# Examples

Use these examples to revise short cards into operational skills.

## Example 1: Causal Design

Weak card: Use natural experiments like top labor economists.

Mature card: Use a real-world rule, threshold, timing shock, or institutional assignment only after stating the estimand and the nearest alternative explanation. The move is to make the comparison credible enough that the reader sees what would have happened otherwise. The card should name the treatment, outcome, population, comparison group, timing, identifying assumption, and strongest threat. The boundary is that a convenient shock does not become causal evidence until the comparison answers the counterfactual question.

Why the mature version works: it changes the researcher's next action. The researcher must define the learning object and threat before choosing the empirical design.

## Example 2: Institutional Taste

Weak card: Study institutions deeply.

Mature card: Translate institutional detail into a changed constraint. Write the mechanism as: because rule X changes constraint Y for agent Z, behavior or outcome W should change. Then identify the comparison that would reveal that channel and the bundled institutional factor that could mimic it.

Why the mature version works: it prevents institutional color from becoming scenery. It turns context into a falsifiable mechanism.

## Example 3: Finance And Market Signals

Weak card: Use prices as evidence.

Mature card: Use prices as evidence only after defining the benchmark and separating candidate interpretations. A return pattern may reflect risk, mispricing, limits to arbitrage, changing discount rates, cash-flow news, liquidity, or data mining. The skill is to ask what additional moment, cross-section, event, balance-sheet measure, or model restriction distinguishes the interpretation the paper wants.

Why the mature version works: it protects against treating a market fact as a mechanism before alternatives are ruled out.

## Example 4: Theory

Weak card: Build an elegant model.

Mature card: Build the simplest benchmark in which the result should not arise, then add one load-bearing departure that changes the prediction, allocation, or welfare comparison. The card should say which assumption carries the result and what would fail if the assumption were removed.

Why the mature version works: it makes the model serve the argument rather than decorate it.

## Example 5: Welfare And Human Decisions

Weak card: Study important social problems.

Mature card: Define the decision problem, constraint, and welfare object before claiming policy relevance. If the project studies poverty, labor, development, or behavior, the card should say what agents choose or lack, what constraint or behavioral force changes the outcome, what evidence distinguishes mechanism from description, and what welfare or policy claim remains too strong.

Why the mature version works: it keeps human importance compatible with economic discipline.
