# Glossary

A taste model is your internal judgment system for research. It is the part of you that decides whether a question is important, whether a mechanism is doing real work, whether a measure captures the concept, whether an empirical design deserves trust, and whether a contribution claim is honest. The model improves only when it receives feedback from reading, writing, testing, and revision.

A skill is a reusable research move. It is more concrete than a principle and more portable than a topic. “Use administrative data” is too broad to be a skill. “Use administrative data to map a hidden mechanism at a scale where small-sample evidence would mislead” is closer, because it tells the researcher what the move is for and when it matters.

A research move is the intellectual action inside a paper: isolating a mechanism, building a measure, finding variation, designing a falsification test, reframing a contribution, or narrowing a claim. Reading for taste means finding these moves and asking whether they can travel.

Evidence is the material that keeps the skill honest. It can be a paper, dataset, research design, author page, lecture, referee exchange, or field history. Without evidence, a skill card becomes advice. With evidence, it becomes a disciplined reading note.

An anti-skill is a habit that looks like sophistication but weakens the paper. Examples include adding robustness checks without learning, mistaking a topic for a contribution, using identification language beyond what the design supports, or making a mechanism sound precise without testing it.
