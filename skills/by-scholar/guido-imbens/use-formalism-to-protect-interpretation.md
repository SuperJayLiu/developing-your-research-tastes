# Use formalism to protect interpretation

## Source Scholar

Guido Imbens

## Generalisable Skill

Use notation to prevent verbal overclaiming, especially when treatment effects are heterogeneous or
assignment is imperfect.

## Evidence Anchors

- Identification and Estimation of Local Average Treatment Effects
- Treatment-effect heterogeneity papers
- Causal inference texts

## Taste Extraction Notes

### What the papers tend to test

The theory shows how the same estimator can answer different questions under different assumptions
and populations.

### What the abstract/introduction tends to do

The introduction often frames the contribution as preventing a common interpretive mistake rather
than simply proposing a new technique.

### Why this skill travels

Use this skill when a result sounds simple but the population or margin is subtle.

### Critical boundary

The risk is that formalism becomes detached from applied stakes. The skill works best when notation
clarifies a real interpretive hazard.

## Practice Exercise

Take one published estimate and identify the population for whom it is most credible.

## How to Use This Skill in Your Own Research

1. Restate your research idea using this skill.
2. Name the theory or mechanism it implies.
3. State one empirical or theoretical test that could reject it.
4. Identify one rival explanation.
5. Write the introduction paragraph that would make the reader care.
